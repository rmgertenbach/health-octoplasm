Healthcare 101

Healthcare is confusing by design. Let's make it simple.
No jargon. No BS. Just what you need to know.

Why Does the Same MRI Cost $400 or $3,000?

5 minute read • The most important thing to understand

The Short Answer: Because They Can

Healthcare prices aren't based on cost or quality. They're based on what providers think they can get away with.

Real Example:

Same MRI scan in Austin, Texas:

  • HighQuality Imaging: $425 (Grade A quality)
  • MegaHealth Hospital: $2,800 (Grade C quality)

Same machine. Same scan. 7x price difference. Worse quality costs more.

Why This Happens

1. You Don't Know the Price

Imagine going to a restaurant where they don't show prices until AFTER you eat. That's healthcare. Providers charge whatever they want because you can't compare.

2. Insurance Hides It

Your insurance pays (most of) it, so you don't care. But guess who pays for insurance? You do - through premiums, deductibles, and lower wages.

3. No Correlation with Quality

Expensive doesn't mean better. Often the opposite. Big hospital systems charge more because they can, not because they're higher quality.

The Good News: Federal transparency laws now require hospitals and insurers to publish their prices. That's what this website uses. Now you can know BEFORE you go.

How Insurance Actually Works (And Why It's Complicated)

3 minute read • Deductibles, coinsurance, and out-of-pocket maximums explained

The Basics

Key Terms:

Premium: What you pay every month just to have insurance. Like a membership fee.
Deductible: How much YOU pay before insurance starts helping. If your deductible is $3,000, you pay the first $3,000 of care yourself.
Coinsurance: After you hit your deductible, you still pay a percentage (usually 20%). Insurance pays the rest (80%).
Out-of-Pocket Maximum: The most you'll ever pay in a year. After this, insurance pays 100%.

Real Example: Getting an MRI

Scenario: You need an MRI. You haven't met your deductible yet.

Option A: MegaHealth Hospital

  • MRI costs: $2,800
  • You pay: $2,800 (toward deductible)
  • Insurance pays: $0
  • Your bill: $2,800

Option B: HighQuality Imaging

  • MRI costs: $425
  • You pay: $425 (toward deductible)
  • Insurance pays: $0
  • Your bill: $425

You saved $2,375 by shopping around. Same scan, same quality (actually better!).

Pro Tip: If you have a high deductible ($3,000+), ask about the CASH price. Sometimes it's cheaper to pay cash directly than use insurance. Yes, really.

When Should You Pay Cash Instead of Using Insurance?

2 minute read • This sounds crazy but can save you thousands

The Math Makes Sense

Hospitals charge insurance companies inflated "negotiated rates." But they offer lower "cash prices" if you pay directly. If the cash price is less than your deductible, pay cash and save money.

When to Pay Cash:

  • You haven't met your deductible yet
  • The cash price is lower than the insurance price
  • You can afford to pay upfront
  • The procedure is non-emergency (you have time to shop)

Real Example:

You need a colonoscopy. You have a $5,000 deductible and haven't met it yet.

Option 1: Use Insurance

  • Insurance "negotiated rate": $4,500
  • You pay all of it (deductible not met): $4,500

Option 2: Pay Cash

  • Cash price: $2,200
  • You pay: $2,200

Saved $2,300 by paying cash. Insurance would have cost you MORE.

Important: Cash payments usually DON'T count toward your deductible. But if you're not going to hit your deductible anyway, who cares? Save the money.

How to Read (and Fight) a Medical Bill

4 minute read • What all those numbers mean and what to do if it's wrong

Understanding Your EOB (Explanation of Benefits)

Your EOB is NOT a bill - it's a summary of what happened. Here's what each column means:

1. Provider Charges: Made-up number (ignore this - it's fantasy)
2. Negotiated Rate: What insurance agreed to pay (this matters)
3. Insurance Paid: What insurance actually paid
4. You Owe: What you need to pay (ONLY number that matters!)

Red Flags: When to Fight Your Bill

Fight the bill if you see:

  • "Out of network" for emergency care (illegal under No Surprises Act)
  • Procedures you didn't receive
  • Multiple charges for the same thing
  • Charges WAY above market rate (check our search tool)
  • Balance billing (provider charging you more than insurance agreed)

How to Fight It

Step-by-step:

  1. DON'T PAY YET. Paying admits the charge is correct.
  2. Call the billing department. Ask for an itemized bill and explanation of each charge.
  3. Compare to market rates. Use our search tool to see if you're being overcharged.
  4. File an appeal. In writing. Keep copies of everything.
  5. Don't give up. Most people pay without fighting. Don't be most people.

Healthcare Cheat Sheet

Print this and keep it in your wallet

Before Any Medical Procedure

1
Ask for the CPT code - That's the billing code. You need it to compare prices.
2
Search our tool - Find 3-5 providers who do that procedure in your area.
3
Ask for cash prices - Many places have lower cash prices than insurance.
4
Get it in writing - Before the procedure, get a written estimate of what you'll owe.
5
Check quality grades - Don't just go cheap. Look for Grade A + fair price = best value.
