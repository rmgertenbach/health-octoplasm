# Healthcare Finance Fundamentals: A Primer for Transparency Innovation

*Based on insights from Mark Cuban (Cost Plus Drugs), Cora Opsahl (32BJ Health Fund), and healthcare finance expert Stacey Richter*

## Executive Overview: The Healthcare Finance Landscape

### The Core Problem
American healthcare operates as what some experts call "a game of Pachinko" - you input a solution, and it bounces unpredictably through perverse incentives, non-market dynamics, and complex intermediaries. The result? A $4.9 trillion sector where solutions often create worse problems than they solve.

### The Three Fundamental Questions
According to Mark Cuban, healthcare business boils down to three simple questions:
1. **How much does it cost?** (Pricing)
2. **How do you pay for it?** (Financing)
3. **Who takes the risk for people who can't pay?** (Risk management)

The industry's complexity stems from our failure to answer these questions transparently.

### Key Stakeholders in Your Work

As someone working in healthcare transparency and data, you'll be serving these stakeholders:

**Self-Insured Employers** (covering ~160 million Americans)
- Take on full financial risk for employee healthcare
- Often lack expertise to manage this effectively
- Your largest opportunity for impact

**Patients/Members**
- Functionally uninsured if deductibles exceed bank balances
- Cannot shop effectively without transparent pricing
- Delay care due to cost uncertainty

**Providers (Hospitals/Physicians)**
- Forced into debt collection role
- Revenue cycle management consumes enormous resources
- Want predictable payment but trapped in complex contracts

**Carriers/TPAs/PBMs**
- Process claims and administer benefits
- Can add value or extract rents depending on contract structure
- Control data flows (your competitive landscape)

## Part 1: Understanding Self-Insured Healthcare

### What Is Self-Insurance?

When a company is "self-insured," they don't actually buy insurance in the traditional sense. Instead:

- **The employer pays all medical claims directly** from company funds
- They hire a carrier (like Blue Cross Blue Shield, United, Cigna, Aetna, Humana - collectively "BUCAH") to provide only administrative services (ASO model)
- The carrier becomes a **Third-Party Administrator (TPA)** who:
  - Negotiates provider contracts (or uses existing networks)
  - Processes claims
  - Manages prior authorizations
  - Handles member services

**Critical Insight**: The employer takes ALL the financial risk. The carrier/TPA takes none. Yet the employer often has minimal visibility into what they're actually paying for.

### Why Companies Self-Insure

1. **Cost Control** (in theory): Avoid insurance company profit margins and risk charges
2. **Flexibility**: Can design benefit plans to match workforce needs
3. **ERISA Protection**: Federal law preempts many state regulations
4. **Cash Flow**: Pay claims as incurred vs. fixed premiums

### The Reality Gap

As Mark Cuban notes: "Any company with 500 or more employees... can afford to pay somebody $150,000 to do just that [manage healthcare] and get a return of their money almost immediately because they just don't realize just how much money they're leaving on the table."

**The problem**: Most employers treat healthcare as a procurement exercise, not the strategic business it actually is. Warren Buffett famously said Ford Motor Company was "an insurance company disguised as a car company" - the same applies to any large self-insured employer.

## Part 2: High-Deductible Health Plans - When Solutions Become Problems

### The Original Logic

High-deductible health plans (HDHPs) were designed with sound economic theory:
- Give consumers "skin in the game"
- Price transparency would drive shopping behavior
- Competition would lower costs
- Moral hazard would decrease

### What Actually Happened

**Creating Functional Uninsurance**
- 40% of Americans have $400 or less in savings
- A $1,500 deductible might as well be $10 million for these families
- Insurance becomes worthless if you can't afford to use it

**Perverse Incentives Cascade**
1. **Delayed Care**: Patients avoid early treatment → more expensive care later
2. **Emergency Room Overuse**: Can't afford primary care → ER for emergencies → uncompensated care rises
3. **Provider Impact**: Doctors become "subprime lenders," forced to:
   - Collect debt before providing care
   - Hire armies of billing staff (1 administrator per 2 providers is now common)
   - Offer in-house financing at potentially usurious rates

**Insurance Company Windfall**
- Higher deductibles = less likely to be met = money stays with carrier
- Even for self-insured employers, carriers benefit from the "float" (holding money before paying claims)
- No real risk taken, but administrative fees continue

### The Transparency Problem

Even with price transparency regulations, patients face:
- **DRG codes** (Diagnosis-Related Groups for inpatient care)
- **CPT codes** (Current Procedural Terminology for outpatient)
- Facility fees, unbundled services, and surprise billing
- No way to know actual out-of-pocket cost in advance

As Cora Opsahl states: "Healthcare is one of the only commodities that you buy with never knowing how much it's gonna cost."

## Part 3: The Intermediary Problem - Complexity Breeding Complexity

### The Layering Effect

To manage healthcare complexity, employers hire:
- **Benefits Consultants/Brokers** (EBCs)
- **TPAs** or **ASO carriers**
- **Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs)**
- **Revenue Cycle Management firms**
- **Cost containment vendors**
- **Navigation/steering companies**

**The paradox**: Each layer added to reduce complexity adds more complexity.

### How Value Leaks Out

**Spread Pricing**
Cynthia Fisher (referenced in the transcript) notes carriers can add ~30% "spread" on top of provider charges. For example:
- Provider charges: $10,000
- Carrier bills employer: $13,000
- Provider receives: $10,000 (or less due to "underpayments")
- Carrier keeps: $3,000+ (or pays affiliated entities)

**Vertical Integration Games**
Consider a major insurance carrier that:
- Owns a PBM (pharmacy benefit manager)
- Owns retail pharmacies
- Owns medical groups
- Owns urgent care centers
- Has 2,500+ subsidiaries
- Processes $161 billion in annual intercompany transfers (0.3% of US GDP!)

They can:
- Pay themselves for services (counts toward 85% MLR requirement)
- Hide profits in subsidiaries
- Negotiate with themselves
- Control all data flows

**MLR (Medical Loss Ratio) Gaming**
The ACA requires carriers to spend 85% of premiums on medical care. But:
- Higher prices = higher 15% administrative allowance
- Owning providers means paying yourself counts as "medical costs"
- Incentive is to increase spending, not decrease it

### Why This Matters for Your Work

Your transparency tools must cut through these layers to show:
- What employers actually pay (not "negotiated discounts")
- What providers actually receive (not billed charges)
- Where value leaks occur (spread pricing, hidden fees)
- What services actually cost to deliver (true cost of care)

## Part 4: Direct Contracting - Disaggregating the System

### What Is Direct Contracting?

Instead of using carrier networks, employers:
1. Negotiate directly with hospitals/physician groups
2. Set transparent, pre-negotiated prices (often cash rates or Medicare-based)
3. Remove carrier/TPA from provider payment
4. May still use TPA for claims processing only

### The Value Proposition

**For Employers:**
- 10-20% immediate savings (Cuban's experience)
- Predictable costs
- No claim underpayments or denials to manage

**For Providers:**
- Cash payment upfront (no collection risk)
- No pre-authorization denials
- Reduced administrative burden (1 admin per 10 providers vs. 1 per 2)
- No "underpayments" where carriers pay less than contracted rates

**For Patients:**
- Often $0 copays (employer can afford it with savings)
- No surprise bills
- No debt collection
- Better access to care

### The 32BJ Case Study

Cora Opsahl's health fund:
- Removed a high-cost hospital from network
- Saved millions in excess charges
- Members got "biggest raise they had ever gotten"
- Employers got premium holiday
- "Almost no member noise" because gave value back to members

**Key lesson**: Cutting costs in healthcare shouldn't be about bottom line alone - it's about redirecting savings to wages, better benefits, or both.

### Why It's Difficult

**Contract Barriers**: Many TPA contracts prevent:
- Employers from accessing their own claims data
- Direct negotiations with providers
- Removing specific facilities from networks

**Network Adequacy**: Employers worry about:
- Geographic coverage
- Access to specialists
- Emergency care scenarios

**Knowledge Gaps**: 
- HR departments lack healthcare expertise
- CFOs don't understand healthcare finance specifics
- No one has time to become expert (dealing with daily crises)

**Provider Capacity**: 
- Hospitals accustomed to carrier contracts
- Billing systems designed for insurance claims
- Risk of volume loss if prices too transparent

### The Reinsurance Piece

Self-insured employers need **stop-loss/reinsurance** to protect against catastrophic claims ($50K+ per member). 

Mark Cuban's approach:
- Less than $500 per family per month for reinsurance starting at $50K
- Employer self-funds everything under $50K threshold
- Makes risk manageable even for smaller companies

## Part 5: The Transparency Challenge - Your Core Work

### Why Transparency Is Hard

**Multiple Price Definitions**
1. **Chargemaster rates**: Fantasy numbers, 3-5x actual payments
2. **Negotiated rates**: Vary wildly by carrier and employer
3. **Cash prices**: Often lower than insured rates
4. **Medicare rates**: Government-set, often used as benchmark
5. **Actual payments**: What providers receive (may be less than "negotiated")

**Data Fragmentation**
- Employers get claims data but not payment data
- Providers see bills sent but not what carriers actually pay
- Patients see EOBs (Explanation of Benefits) that are incomprehensible
- Quality data lives separately from cost data

**Deliberate Obfuscation**
- 40 pages of contract disclaimers
- Non-disclosure agreements
- Proprietary algorithms
- Bundling and unbundling services
- Facility fees appearing unexpectedly

### What Real Transparency Requires

**1. Pricing Transparency**
- True contracted rates (not "discounts from chargemaster")
- Out-of-pocket costs before service
- Total episode costs (not just facility fees)
- Cash-equivalent prices

**2. Quality Transparency**
- Outcomes data by provider
- Complication rates
- Patient satisfaction
- Leapfrog scores and other safety ratings
- Appropriateness of care

**3. Contract Transparency**
- What TPAs are actually paid
- Hidden fees and spread pricing
- Performance guarantees
- Termination clauses and data rights

**4. Claims Flow Transparency**
- What employers pay
- What providers receive
- Where money goes in between
- Time to payment

### Regulatory Tools (Your Potential Data Sources)

**CAA (Consolidated Appropriations Act)**
- Machine-readable files (MRFs) with negotiated rates
- Required from all carriers/TPAs
- Massive data sets but difficult to parse

**Hospital Price Transparency Rules**
- Hospitals must publish chargemaster and negotiated rates
- Shoppable services with cash prices
- Enforcement weak but data increasingly available

**APCDs (All-Payer Claims Databases)**
- State-level claims databases
- Aggregate data across payers
- Some states have robust systems (CO, NH, VA, etc.)

**No Surprises Act**
- Good faith estimates required
- Advanced EOBs
- Creates data on expected vs. actual costs

## Part 6: Real-World Scenarios - Applying This Knowledge

### Scenario 1: The High-Deductible Trap

**Situation**: 
ABC Manufacturing (1,200 employees) has a $3,000 individual deductible plan. HR is getting complaints that employees can't afford to fill prescriptions or see specialists. The carrier says this is "normal healthcare utilization" and projects 8% premium increase next year.

**Problem Analysis**:
- Employee median wage: $45,000
- 40% of workforce has < $500 in savings
- $3,000 deductible = functionally uninsured for nearly half of workforce
- Delayed care will create more expensive claims later
- Carrier still gets administrative fees regardless of utilization
- The 8% increase is for carrier's book of business, not ABC's actual experience

**Transparency Questions You'd Ask**:
1. What is ABC's actual claims experience vs. carrier's book?
2. What percentage of employees meet their deductible?
3. What conditions are going untreated until they become emergencies?
4. What are the cash prices at local providers vs. "negotiated rates"?
5. How much is the carrier's administrative fee vs. what a direct contract would cost?

**Potential Solutions**:
- Implement $0 copay for preventive care and chronic disease management
- Direct contract with primary care practices for unlimited visits
- Use Mark Cuban Cost Plus Drugs for pharmacy (radical price reduction)
- Lower deductible but increase HSA contribution to offset
- Create transparency tools showing total cost of care paths

**Your Role**: Build tools that show ABC:
- True cost per episode vs. what carrier reports
- Employee utilization patterns (are high deductibles suppressing needed care?)
- Local market cash pricing vs. their "negotiated discounts"
- ROI modeling for direct contracting options

### Scenario 2: The Black Box RFP

**Situation**:
XYZ Union Health Fund (50,000 members) goes to RFP for medical carrier and PBM. They receive proposals from 4 major carriers. All promise "8-12% savings through our high-performance network and utilization management." The benefits consultant recommends the lowest bid.

**Problem Analysis**:
- "Savings" are measured against what benchmark? Last year's inflated spend?
- "High-performance network" may just mean higher copays, not better quality
- Carrier doing the RFP may also own PBM (negotiating with themselves)
- Consultant may receive undisclosed commissions from carriers
- No visibility into actual provider payment rates
- "Utilization management" often means denial management (delaying payment)

**Hidden Costs**:
- Spread pricing on both medical and pharmacy
- "Rebates" on drugs that don't get passed through
- Network "discounts" off inflated chargemaster rates
- Underpayments to providers (carrier pays less than contracted rate)
- Administrative fees hidden in per-member-per-month charges

**Transparency Questions You'd Ask**:
1. What are the actual contracted rates with specific hospitals?
2. How do these compare to Medicare rates? Cash prices? NADAC for drugs?
3. What does the carrier pay providers vs. what XYZ pays the carrier?
4. Is the consultant getting paid by carriers? How much?
5. What contract terms prevent XYZ from direct contracting or switching?
6. What percentage of claims get denied? How long until payment?
7. What are the true admin costs vs. medical spend?

**What Good Looks Like (Cora's Advice)**:
- Demand pass-through PBM pricing (transparent, paid flat fee)
- Require contract that allows direct provider negotiations
- Insist on full claims data ownership
- Get transparent provider payment reports
- Include performance guarantees with penalties
- Use open-source contract templates (available free from experts)

**Your Role**: Build RFP evaluation tools that:
- Normalize "savings" claims to actual market benchmarks
- Flag contract terms that lock in bad economics
- Model total cost of ownership (not just premiums)
- Compare network prices to transparent alternatives
- Identify conflicts of interest in consultant relationships

### Scenario 3: The Direct Contracting Decision

**Situation**:
TechCo (800 employees in Austin, TX) wants to explore direct contracting after hearing about potential 15-20% savings. CFO is interested but concerned about disruption. HR is overwhelmed and worried about employee complaints. Consultant says "it's too risky."

**Current State**:
- Fully self-insured through Blue Cross ASO
- $8M annual healthcare spend (second-largest expense after payroll)
- Standard $2,000 deductible, 80/20 coinsurance
- Network includes all major Austin hospitals
- 12% increase projected for next year

**Decision Framework**:

**Step 1: Analyze Current Economics**
Questions to answer with data:
- What is TechCo actually paying per service vs. market rates?
- Which providers drive most spend? (80/20 rule applies)
- What are Medicare rates for those services?
- What spread is Blue Cross charging?
- What would direct cash prices be?

**Step 2: Assess Feasibility**
Network considerations:
- Austin has robust hospital competition (unlike rural markets)
- Can cover 80% of care with 2-3 direct contracts
- Need wraparound network for complex cases (Blue Cross offers $18 PMPM)
- Emergency coverage through existing insurance for catastrophic

**Step 3: Design Transition**
Phased approach Mark Cuban recommends:
- Year 1: Direct contract for imaging and labs (easy, high-volume, commoditized)
  - Immediate 30-40% savings
  - Low disruption (employees likely don't care which imaging center)
  - Proof of concept
  
- Year 2: Add primary care and routine specialty care
  - Partner with independent practices or smaller systems
  - Offer $0 copays to drive utilization
  - Pre-authorize everything (remove provider risk)
  
- Year 3: Add major hospital for scheduled procedures
  - Focus on high-quality, lower-cost options
  - Bundle pricing for episodes (hip replacement = $X total, not itemized)
  - Keep expensive academic center for complex cases only

**Step 4: Risk Mitigation**
- Maintain reinsurance (stop-loss at $50K per member)
- Keep Blue Cross wraparound network for travel/emergencies
- Implement strong member communication (emphasize savings → higher wages)
- Hire or contract healthcare CFO ($150K salary, saves $1.2M+ annually)

**Expected Outcomes**:
- 15-20% savings = $1.2-1.6M annually
- Can fund: Healthcare CFO salary + higher wages + lower deductibles
- Reduced administrative burden on HR (fewer denials to fight)
- Better employee satisfaction (lower out-of-pocket, better access)

**Your Role**: Create decision support tools that:
- Model financial scenarios (savings vs. implementation costs)
- Map provider landscape (quality + pricing)
- Benchmark current spending to direct contract alternatives
- Simulate employee impact (access, out-of-pocket changes)
- Track savings realization over time

### Scenario 4: The Pricing Failure Discovery

**Situation**:
HealthyState Union Fund discovers they're paying $42,000 for a hip replacement at MegaHealth System when HighQuality Orthopedics offers the same procedure for $18,000 (both excellent Leapfrog scores). Their carrier/TPA negotiated the MegaHealth contract and won't explain the price difference.

**Problem Analysis**:
This is what Stacey Richter calls "pricing failure" - where expense is really just bad pricing, not actual resource consumption.

**Why This Happens**:
1. **Market Power**: MegaHealth has consolidated market → monopoly pricing
2. **Contract Opacity**: Carrier negotiates with MegaHealth across entire book of business (not optimizing for HealthyState)
3. **Facility Fees**: MegaHealth charges "facility fees" that independent HighQuality cannot
4. **Incentive Misalignment**: Higher prices = higher carrier administrative fees (percentage-based)
5. **Provider Mix**: MegaHealth may give carrier better rates on other services → bundled negotiation
6. **Steering Failure**: Members go to MegaHealth because "it's in-network" (cost invisible)

**Transparency Reveals**:
- MegaHealth's Medicare rate: $15,000
- HighQuality's direct contract price: $18,000  
- HighQuality's insured rate: $22,000
- MegaHealth's insured rate: $42,000
- MegaHealth's chargemaster: $86,000 (meaningless, but shows "50% discount")

**The T-Shirt Analogy (Cora's)**:
"Why pay Nordstrom prices for a Target t-shirt? If they're both white t-shirts (same quality), I should pay Target prices."

But current system:
- Can't see quality data (which is the "white t-shirt"?)
- Can't see true prices (just "discounts" from fake chargemaster)
- Steered to expensive option by insurance design
- Consultant/TPA may have financial relationship with expensive provider

**Solutions**:
1. **Remove MegaHealth from network** (Cora's approach)
   - Requires contract flexibility
   - Strong member communication
   - Offer transportation to HighQuality if needed
   - Share savings with members (higher wages, lower premiums)

2. **Create Financial Incentives**
   - $0 copay at HighQuality
   - $5,000 copay at MegaHealth
   - Make price difference visible

3. **Direct Contract**
   - Negotiate $16,000 bundled rate with HighQuality
   - Pay cash upfront
   - Remove all member cost-sharing
   - Everyone wins (except MegaHealth)

**Your Role**: Build tools that:
- Compare provider prices across quality tiers
- Flag "pricing failures" where cost ≠ value
- Model savings from network optimization
- Show member impact of steering strategies
- Benchmark local market rates

## Part 7: Key Concepts for Your Daily Work

### Understanding Claims Data

**What Claims Tell You**:
- Services provided (CPT/DRG codes)
- Provider identity (NPI - National Provider Identifier)
- Billed charges (usually meaningless)
- Allowed amounts (contracted rate)
- Plan paid (what carrier/employer paid)
- Member paid (deductible, coinsurance, copay)

**What Claims Don't Tell You**:
- Actual provider payment (may be less than "plan paid")
- Quality of care
- Appropriateness of care
- Cash equivalent prices
- Episode-level costs (bundled services)

### Price Benchmarks

**Medicare**: 
- Government-set rates
- Generally cover provider costs but not lavish profits
- Canadian hospitals operate profitably at 30-40% less than US Medicare rates
- Useful baseline but politically charged

**Cash Prices**:
- Often 40-60% less than insured rates
- Show what providers will accept without insurance overhead
- Mark Cuban: "Any schmo with a credit card can walk into a hospital and pay the cash rate"

**Reference-Based Pricing**:
- Set payment at Medicare + X% (often 140-180% of Medicare)
- Tell providers: "This is what we'll pay, take it or leave it"
- Requires member protection from balance billing

**NADAC (National Average Drug Acquisition Cost)**:
- What pharmacies actually pay for drugs
- Transparent benchmark for pharmacy pricing
- Massive spread between NADAC and what PBMs charge

### Financial Flows

**Traditional Insured Model**:
```
Member premium → Insurance Carrier → Provider
                ↓
            (keeps profit + admin fees + float)
```

**Self-Insured with ASO**:
```
Employer funds → Carrier/TPA (processes) → Provider
                      ↓
                  (admin fees + spread pricing)
```

**Direct Contract Model**:
```
Employer → Provider (cash payment)
    ↓
Simple TPA (claims processing only, flat fee)
    ↓
Reinsurance (catastrophic only)
```

### Risk Distribution

**Fully Insured**:
- Carrier takes all risk
- Employer pays fixed premium
- Carrier keeps premium if claims low, covers losses if claims high

**Self-Insured**:
- Employer takes all risk up to stop-loss threshold
- Pays actual claims + admin fees
- Saves money when healthy, at risk when sick
- Reinsurance covers catastrophic (>$50K-$250K per member)

**Direct Contract**:
- Employer takes claims risk (same as self-insured)
- Provider takes NO collection risk (paid upfront)
- Carrier takes NO risk (just processor)
- Most efficient risk allocation

## Part 8: How to Think Like a Healthcare Finance Expert

### The Three Questions Framework

For any healthcare finance situation, always ask:

**1. How much does it cost?** (Pricing)
- What's the actual resource consumption?
- What's the market price?
- What's the monopoly rent extraction?
- What's the administrative overhead?

**2. How do you pay for it?** (Financing)
- Who bears the risk?
- What are the payment terms?
- Where is value leaking?
- What are the incentive structures?

**3. Who takes the risk for nonpayment?** (Risk Management)
- Can patients afford their share?
- Are providers forced into lending?
- Is risk appropriately priced?
- Should taxpayers backstop?

### The Transparency Test

Good transparency should enable:
1. **Shopping**: Can a patient compare options before care?
2. **Understanding**: Can a layperson comprehend the price?
3. **Trust**: Is the price connected to actual costs?
4. **Action**: Can stakeholders make different choices with the information?

If transparency doesn't enable these four things, it's compliance theater, not real transparency.

### The Simplicity Principle

Mark Cuban's vision: "Back to 1955, where you can pay with a credit card, cash, or a chicken."

What this means:
- Post prices that mean something
- Eliminate middlemen who don't add value
- Make credit available (with reasonable terms) for those who need it
- Remove billing complexity that serves no one

"If you need revenue cycle management consultants, that's proof of system failure."

### The Power Analysis

Cora Opsahl: "Where does the power lie?"

In any healthcare situation, map the power:
- **Patients**: Almost none (can't shop, can't choose, can't afford)
- **Providers**: Declining (except consolidated systems)
- **Employers**: Have power but don't use it (lack knowledge)
- **Carriers/PBMs**: Maximum power (control data and payment flows)
- **Regulators**: Increasing but still weak enforcement

**Your opportunity**: Shift power through transparency
- Give employers data to negotiate
- Give patients information to choose
- Give providers alternatives to carrier dominance
- Force carriers to compete on value, not opacity

### The Incentive Alignment Check

Ask: "Who benefits if costs go up?"

**Misaligned Incentives**:
- MLR rules: Higher spending = higher admin dollar allowance
- Fee-for-service: More services = more revenue
- Percentage-based fees: Higher prices = higher consultant compensation
- Vertical integration: Carriers paying themselves

**Aligned Incentives**:
- Direct contracts with bundled pricing: Provider keeps savings
- Pass-through PBM with flat fee: PBM wants lowest drug cost
- Self-insured employers: Lower costs = higher wages
- Capitated/value-based care: Provider rewarded for keeping people healthy

## Part 9: Critical Insights for Your Product Work

### What Healthcare Transparency Products Must Do

**1. Make the Invisible Visible**
- True contracted rates (not "discounts")
- Provider payment vs. employer payment (spread)
- Quality alongside price
- Total episode costs (not unbundled)
- Administrative costs vs. clinical costs

**2. Enable Action, Not Just Information**
- "Here's a cheaper option" (with quality data)
- "Here's a provider willing to direct contract"
- "Here's where your contract has escape clauses"
- "Here's your expected out-of-pocket before you commit"

**3. Cut Through Complexity**
- No one understands DRG codes - translate to plain English
- No one can evaluate 1,000-page contracts - highlight key terms
- No one has time for deep analysis - provide actionable insights

**4. Build Trust**
- Independent from carriers/PBMs (no conflicts)
- Auditable methodologies
- Clear about data limitations
- Protected from retaliation

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**1. Transparency Theater**
- Publishing machine-readable files no one can read
- Showing chargemaster rates that mean nothing
- Providing quality scores without price context
- Giving information without decision support

**2. Overwhelming Users**
- Too much data, too little insight
- Requiring healthcare finance expertise to interpret
- Multiple tools that don't integrate
- Analysis without clear recommendations

**3. Misaligned Incentives**
- Taking payments from providers you rate
- Revenue from "steering" to specific networks
- Selling data back to carriers you're trying to disrupt
- Optimizing for engagement vs. outcomes

**4. Ignoring Implementation Reality**
- Great analysis, no action plan
- Solutions that require contract changes employers can't make
- Recommendations that ignore local market dynamics
- Tools that require behavior change without support

### Questions Your Tools Should Answer

**For Employers**:
- Are we paying competitive rates or being overcharged?
- Which providers deliver best value (quality per dollar)?
- Where should we direct contract first?
- What contract terms are costing us money?
- How much are our intermediaries really costing?
- What would our spend be with full transparency?

**For Patients**:
- What will this actually cost me out of pocket?
- Are there cheaper options with equal quality?
- Is my doctor/hospital safe and effective?
- Can I afford this, and if not, what are my options?
- Should I use insurance or pay cash?

**For Providers**:
- What are fair market rates for our services?
- Which payers are underpaying us?
- How much does billing complexity cost us?
- What would direct contracting be worth?
- How do our prices/quality compare to competitors?

When Cora Opsahl says "You can't fix what you can't see," she's describing your entire mandate. 

When Mark Cuban talks about getting back to 1955 simplicity, he's describing your north star.

When Stacey Richter discusses "pricing failure," she's identifying your primary target.

The question isn't whether healthcare can change - it's whether we'll build the tools that make change inevitable.

That's your job. Welcome to healthcare.
